Post-Overseas: Discovering the Philippines as a Filipino

I look Filipino. Well of course, by blood I am. People speak to me like I am a local, every joke, every words, every place. On the outside, I just blend easily. But things are different on the inside, each experience is something new. Born in a different country, I was raised in a place far from the country Philippines, and now standing in the same country my parents were born. Every second I experience in this country feels like trying to learn a culture that was always meant to be mine. It felt like meeting home for the first time in my life.

John Gabrielle Valencerina
Thursday, January 15, 2026
5 min read

I am grateful. Grateful for having my tongue used to "Tagalog", a language used by Filipinos, while in the country where a different culture and language is everywhere. It was something I carried with me as it is home-spoken. As I wander through the city of Manila, knowing every word spoken, every letter written, and every culture heard from stories, is something that feels surreal. Unlike feeling home is constrained to a building, everywhere became a place of belonging.

One thing I had to learn arriving is the way how people travel. First of all, travelling is not my thing, but if it is needed then please, let me experience it with someone before I use it alone. In my opinion, the first and recommended commute above all else for me is using the train than the Jeepneys.

"Para po!"

The word that helped me throughout my commutes. Every time I travel, I stare at the GPS maps on my phone and timing my line to stop the vehicle. It is a simple word, yes, but it carries weight. Saying it too early feels embarrassing, and too late would feel like a mistake. Either I try to follow along with others in stop areas, or I try to gather myself and say it in the middle of a road where I needed to be.

About the food, doesn't change much. Parents used to cook local dishes throughout my life. The thing is that, even if pork is so common, I would not get used to it every time it is served to my plate. Fried food stalls are second to it. Oily foods are so common that it makes me want to just eat vegetables for life. Fresh fishes on the other hand are one of the best blessings that this country has to offer.

I know I'm not the only one who experienced this especially to those friends or comrades that are yet to return, but what I can say is that life is an act of adapting. It includes places, culture, and people that were always meant to feel like home.
